# Reading Path: For Journalists & Editors

This is not another AI explainer. It is a father's letter to his daughter on the eve of China's most consequential exam, written by an AI practitioner who decided to stop explaining the technology and start explaining what it means. Here is what makes the story different, and where to find the best material.

---

## Stop 1: The Letter Exchange — The Emotional Anchor

Start here for the human story. The author's teenage daughter was the book's first reader. Her unedited feedback is sharp, funny, and occasionally brutal ("Who was it that said they were bad at writing?"). His reply --- "One Leaf Higher" --- ends with the line that captures the book's thesis: "AI can do everything within the distribution. What you can give this world lies outside the distribution." This exchange is the most quotable, most filmable element in the book. It works as a standalone piece.

Read the full exchange: [Letter (English)](/letter) | [Letter (Chinese)](/letter/zh)

**Quotable:** "I don't ask you to be far ahead of everyone else. I only ask that you stand one leaf higher. If you can do that, all the sunlight and rain in the sky are yours."

## Stop 2: The Invisible Dragon (隐龙) — Sagan Meets AI Evaluation

Named after Carl Sagan's dragon-in-the-garage thought experiment: if you cannot measure it, you cannot learn from it. The chapter reframes AI's core challenge --- how do we define what a "good answer" is? --- as an epistemological problem, not just an engineering one. This is strong material for science-and-philosophy coverage. The Sagan connection gives Western audiences an immediate foothold into what is otherwise a distinctly Chinese intellectual framework.

Read more: [Invisible Dragon as Loss Function](/wiki/bridges/invisible-dragon-is-loss-function)

## Stop 3: The Affluent Society (丰饶社会) — The Love Letter Problem

The chapter's opening hook: when AI writes a love letter ten times better than you can, is that augmentation or forgery? This single question --- the distinction between AI doing work you could not do alone and AI pretending to be you --- is the sharpest framing of the authenticity debate in the current AI discourse. It plays well for features, opinion pieces, and debate formats.

**Quotable concept:** "Intelligence inflation --- when 80 becomes the new zero."

Read more: [Intelligence Inflation](/wiki/concepts/intelligence-inflation) | [Augmentation vs. Forgery](/wiki/concepts/augmentation-vs-forgery)

## Stop 4: The Preface — The "Why" Behind the Book

The book exists because a father watched the technology he builds reshape the world his daughter is about to enter. He is not a philosopher or a policy analyst --- he is a practitioner who decided his daughter needed a map. The preface sets up the dual identity that makes this book unusual: insider technical knowledge delivered through the literary and philosophical tradition of classical China. The chapters are named after wuxia martial arts techniques and mythological artifacts --- not as decoration, but as the book's structural architecture.

## Stop 5: The Chen Tianqiao Endorsement — Credibility Anchor

Chen Tianqiao --- founder of Shanda Group, one of China's most prominent tech entrepreneurs turned brain-science philanthropist --- endorsed the book. His involvement signals that this is taken seriously within China's tech establishment, not just in literary or educational circles. Useful for establishing credibility in pitches and introductions.

## Story Angles

- **The father-daughter angle**: AI practitioner writes survival guide for his humanities-track teenager. Her feedback is the authenticity proof.
- **East meets West**: Wuxia fiction and Daoist philosophy as structural frameworks for understanding AI --- not cultural decoration, but genuine intellectual architecture.
- **The love letter problem**: A single thought experiment that reframes the entire AI-and-jobs debate around authenticity rather than productivity.
- **Intelligence inflation**: When 80 becomes the new zero, the definition of scarcity shifts from production to perspective.
