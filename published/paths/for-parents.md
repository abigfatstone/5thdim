# Reading Path: For Parents

This book was written by a father for his daughter on the eve of China's gaokao --- the college entrance exam that determines the trajectory of 13 million students each year. If you have been wondering what to tell your kids about AI, start here. Someone has already had that conversation, and he wrote it down.

---

## Stop 1: The Preface — Why a Father Wrote This Book

The book opens with a parent's urgency. Xun Jiang is an AI practitioner who watched the technology he builds reshape the world his daughter is about to enter. He did not want to hand her a textbook. He wanted to give her a framework --- a way of seeing that would hold up regardless of which specific tools exist five years from now. Read the preface to understand the emotional stakes behind the technical content. This book is not neutral analysis; it is a father trying to equip his child.

## Stop 2: The Affluent Society (丰饶社会) — What Becomes Scarce When Everything Is Abundant

This is the chapter that reframes the anxiety. When AI lifts everyone's abilities to 80 out of 100, 80 becomes the new zero. The chapter opens with the "love letter problem": when AI writes a love letter better than your child can, is that help or fraud? The distinction between AI-as-augmentation (analyzing data your child could never process alone) and AI-as-forgery (pretending the output is theirs) is one every parent needs to understand. The scarcity has moved --- from the ability to produce to the ability to be authentic.

Read more: [Intelligence Inflation](/wiki/concepts/intelligence-inflation) | [Augmentation vs. Forgery](/wiki/concepts/augmentation-vs-forgery)

## Stop 3: The Sinew Transformation Classic (易筋经) — What Your Child Should Train

Forget the debate about which major to choose. This chapter offers a sharper lens: **verification asymmetry**. Tasks that are easy to verify get automated. Tasks that are hard to verify --- judgment, taste, strategic thinking, ethical reasoning --- become the new scarcity. The implication for parenting is direct: encourage your children to develop the skills that resist measurement, not the ones that optimize for it. The test score is easy to verify. The ability to ask the right question is not.

Read more: [Verification Asymmetry](/wiki/concepts/verification-asymmetry) | [Sinew Classic as Skill Retraining](/wiki/bridges/sinew-classic-is-skill-retraining)

## Stop 4: The Letter Exchange — The Conversation You Wish You Could Have

The daughter's unedited feedback is funny, sharp, and occasionally dismissive ("sorry not sorry, lol"). The father's reply is tender and precise. He tells her: "I don't ask you to be far ahead of everyone else. I only ask that you stand one leaf higher." He does not tell her what to study. He tells her where to look. If you are struggling to talk to your own teenager about the future, this exchange is a template --- not for the answers, but for the tone.

## Stop 5: Change and Constants — What Endures

The final chapter distills three principles that have governed every major technological shift in history: the simple defeats the complex, the flowing defeats the hoarded, the self-evolving defeats the static. These are not AI-specific insights --- they apply to how you raise a child, how you build a career, how you navigate uncertainty. This chapter is the long view, and it is the most reassuring part of the book. The principles that matter do not change with the technology.
