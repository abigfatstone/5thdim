# Author Profile: Xun Jiang

Xun Jiang did not set out to write a book. He set out to write a letter.

In early 2020, as the world was shutting down, Jiang drafted the prologue of what he envisioned as a sequel to Nicholas Negroponte's *Being Digital* -- a new map for a world where bits had already won. His ambition was vast, but his honesty was vaster: he realized his knowledge could not yet support the vision, and he set the manuscript aside.

Five years passed. Jiang spent them as an AI practitioner in China, working alongside Chen Tianqiao, the founder of Shanda Interactive Entertainment and now a figure at the intersection of brain science and artificial intelligence. It was an education in deep data over big data, in asking not "how much do we know" but "how well do we know it." The ideas accumulated. The book waited.

Then came the crisis at the end of 2025. Jiang describes the pain as so intense that "heartbreak" stopped being a metaphor and became a physiological fact. But it was precisely that pain that broke through the inertia. Once the worst had passed, he picked up his pen again -- this time with an urgent, specific audience: his daughter, Yanzi, three months away from the gaokao, China's grueling college entrance exam.

Yanzi is a humanities student. She hates probability. She cannot keep expected values straight. She chose the liberal arts track in her first year of high school and told her father flatly: "I don't want to work in the industry." Jiang was surprised, and a little disappointed. But he understood something important -- if he could not explain the age of AI to a math-averse teenager who had no interest in joining the field, he could not explain it to anyone.

So he wrote the book for her. He named each chapter after a spell from Chinese wuxia fiction -- the Shadow Clone, the Star-Absorbing Skill, the Sinew Transformation Classic -- turning loss functions and autoregressive prediction into martial arts techniques a high schooler could grasp. He drew on Zhuangzi's concept of "the usefulness of the useless," the Diamond Sutra's teaching that all conditioned phenomena are like dreams and illusions, and the Daoist principle that the Way follows nature. These were not decorative references; they were the book's structural architecture.

The result is *The Fifth Dimension: Amplified Choices -- Survival Leverage in the Age of AI*. Its central provocation: when AI lifts everyone's abilities to 80 out of 100, 80 becomes the new zero. The standard answer is now commodity output. What remains scarce is not the ability to produce what others cannot, but the ability to offer what no one else can -- to become, statistically speaking, *outside the distribution*.

When Yanzi read the manuscript, she skipped every math problem. She told her father she finally understood what entropy means. She said the "effect before cause" example was cool -- "you know, where you get killed first and *then* see that you're about to get killed." She wrote, "the information world makes entropy more concise," a sentence her father says is better than his entire book.

Jiang co-created *The Fifth Dimension* with AI, and he is transparent about it. "A thousand people have access to the same AI," he writes in the acknowledgements, "yet a thousand people can write a thousand different Hamlets." For him, the collaboration was not a shortcut but a proof of concept: the book's own existence demonstrates its thesis. AI handles everything within the distribution. What it cannot supply is the private context, the taste, the lived experience that makes one person's Hamlet different from another's.

If he could leave his daughter only one signal, it would be this: "What you can give this world lies outside the distribution." One leaf higher is enough -- your own leaf.
